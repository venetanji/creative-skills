# Suno Lyrics Guide

## Section Tags

Place these on their own line before each section. Suno uses them to structure the song arrangement.

| Tag | Purpose |
|-----|---------|
| `[Verse]` / `[Verse 2]` | Main storytelling sections |
| `[Chorus]` | Repeated hook — the emotional peak |
| `[Pre-Chorus]` | Build-up before the chorus |
| `[Bridge]` | Contrasting section, usually once near the end |
| `[Intro]` | Opening section |
| `[Outro]` | Closing section |
| `[Hook]` | Short repeated phrase |
| `[Instrumental]` | Instrumental break — no vocals here |
| `[Solo]` | Instrumental solo (guitar, sax, etc.) |
| `[Break]` | Rhythmic or percussive break |
| `[Interlude]` | Transitional passage |
| `[Spoken]` | Spoken word delivery (not sung) |
| `[Whispered]` | Whispered vocal delivery |
| `[Ad lib]` | Free improvised vocal fills |
| `[Fade out]` | Song fades at the end |

## Vocal Direction (inline hints)

Add these inside the lyrics to shape delivery:

- `(soft)`, `(loud)`, `(whispering)`, `(screaming)` — volume/intensity
- `(harmonies)`, `(echo)`, `(choir)` — texture
- `(spoken)`, `(rapped)`, `(chanted)` — delivery style
- `(male vocal)`, `(female vocal)`, `(duet)` — voice type

## Example

```
[Intro]
(soft guitar ambient)

[Verse]
Salt in the air and rust on the crane
the fishermen leave before the morning train
old radios hum through the fog and the grey
singing the names of the ones who stayed

[Chorus]
Glass harbour glass harbour
where the cold light bends
where the sea keeps all the secrets
that the shore pretends

[Instrumental]

[Verse 2]
A lighthouse blinks its one-eyed prayer
a child draws boats on the kitchen stair

[Bridge]
(whispered)
everything returns to the water
everything returns

[Chorus]
Glass harbour glass harbour
where the cold light bends
where the sea keeps all the secrets
that the shore pretends

[Outro]
(fade out)
```

## Mechanics

- Keep lines to a natural spoken length — Suno matches syllables to melody.
- **No mid-line punctuation.** Use line breaks instead. Apostrophes and hyphenated compounds (`one-eyed`, `Sessylu's`) are fine; commas, em-dashes, and semicolons inside a line confuse Suno's phrasing.
- Rhyme schemes don't have to be strict — half-rhymes (*moon* / *home*, *line* / *time*) often land better than full rhymes.
- Repeat `[Chorus]` with the *exact* same text — that's how it becomes a hook.
- Use `[Instrumental]` for breathing room between dense sections.
- A full song needs `[Chorus]` at least twice; three times is more conventional.

## Craft (the part most agents skip)

Suno will set whatever you write — but an unmoored prompt produces unmoored lyrics. The five moves below are the difference between "AI-sounding" and lyrics worth listening to.

### 1. Make the line *sound*

Pick a sound and stitch it through the line. Alliteration (initial consonants), assonance (vowels), consonance (interior consonants) — they don't all have to be obvious. The reader doesn't have to *hear* "alliteration" — they just notice the line lands harder.

| ✅ Sound-stitched | ❌ Flat |
|---|---|
| *the wick walks white into the wax and stops* | *the candle is burning down* |
| *fleet folds west like cloth into a chest* | *the ships sail back home* |
| *her name leaves my mouth like steam* | *I forget her name* |

### 2. One specific image beats three abstract ones

A single concrete detail you can see, smell, or touch carries more meaning than three universal statements. Avoid `every`, `the only`, `because every X is Y` — that's aphorism, not lyric. Pick the smallest particular thing and let the reader infer the rest.

| ✅ Particular | ❌ General |
|---|---|
| *fathers who have not yet lost their boys* | *the soldiers go home to their families* |
| *salt on the back of a single wave* | *salt on the open sea* |
| *just a man and a board* | *just a man alone in the ocean* |

### 3. Figurative language: real metaphor, not metaphor-shaped phrases

A real metaphor *transfers a property* from one domain to another with conviction. A bad metaphor announces itself (`like a bird I will fly`) or grafts an emotion-word onto a noun (`the lonely sky`). Extended similes — comparisons that unfold over a line or two — are stronger than one-word adjective swaps.

| ✅ Real metaphor | ❌ Decorative |
|---|---|
| *the rope unwinds into the hemp field where it grew* | *the rope is old and tired* |
| *the gate of the city eats its smoke back in* | *the destroyed city is restored* |
| *I made the mouth that shaped my name* | *I am the architect of my own legend* |

### 4. Don't telegraph the meaning

The lyric is meant to be *experienced*, not summarised. If you write the takeaway, the listener has nothing to do. Trust the image. The bridge is where you can be most direct — not the verses.

| ✅ Trusts the image | ❌ Spelled-out |
|---|---|
| *the wives I wronged become themselves at dawn* | *I have hurt people and now I want forgiveness* |
| *the dogs are dreaming someone else's name* | *I have been forgotten by everyone I loved* |

### 5. Vary the chorus' surroundings, keep the chorus exact

Every entry into the chorus from a different verse should feel like a different door into the same room. The verses' last line is what controls how the chorus lands — make each pre-chorus pivot earn it. The chorus text itself stays bit-identical across repetitions (Suno needs that to recognise it as the hook).

### A worked example — building one good line

Goal: the moment the hero, sailing home, realises the war is behind him.

- **Draft 1 (flat):** *We were sailing home from the war we had won.*
- **Draft 2 (added image):** *We were sailing home and the war was behind us.* — still tells.
- **Draft 3 (image + reversal):** *the songs we sang at sea unsing themselves.*
- **Draft 4 (image + reversal + sound):** *the songs we sang at sea unsing themselves* (the s/s repetition does most of the work — leave it).

Three drafts to get from prose summary to a line that actually sounds like a song.
